SSF vs EPF vs CIT: Which Retirement Deduction Should You Claim?

Calculator All Articles

Updated July 2026 · 5 min read

Most salaried Nepalis have their retirement contributions handled automatically by their employer's payroll, but the scheme it flows into — SSF, EPF, or CIT — affects both your take-home pay and how much tax relief you get. Here's the difference in plain terms.

Social Security Fund (SSF)

SSF is the newer, government-run scheme that most formal-sector employers have shifted to. Both employer and employee contribute a percentage of basic salary into the fund, which covers a bundle of benefits — provident fund, gratuity, medical, accident, unemployment, and pension insurance — under one umbrella. If you're an SSF contributor, you're generally exempt from the 1% Social Security Tax that would otherwise apply to your first income slab, since you're already paying into social security directly.

Employees Provident Fund (EPF)

EPF is the older, longer-standing retirement scheme, still used by many government offices, older private companies, and organisations that haven't transitioned to SSF. It functions purely as a provident fund — a portion of salary is deducted and matched by the employer, accumulating with interest until retirement. It doesn't bundle in the other insurances that SSF does.

Citizen Investment Trust (CIT)

CIT is a voluntary retirement and investment scheme people can contribute to on top of, or instead of, EPF/SSF — common among freelancers, business owners, and anyone without an employer-run scheme. Contributions are voluntary and self-directed.

How they affect your tax

For tax purposes, what matters most is that contributions to SSF, EPF, and CIT are combined and deducted from your gross income before slab rates are applied — up to a cap of the lowest of: your actual contribution, NPR 500,000, or one-third of your gross income. Contributing more than that cap doesn't buy you extra tax relief, even though it still grows your retirement savings.

Worked example

Gross income: NPR 1,200,000. Combined SSF contribution: NPR 150,000.
One-third of gross income is NPR 400,000, and the fixed cap is NPR 500,000 — so the full NPR 150,000 is deductible, since it's the smallest of the three figures.
Taxable income becomes NPR 1,050,000 instead of NPR 1,200,000.

Which one should you choose?

If your employer already enrols you in SSF, you generally don't need to duplicate contributions elsewhere — SSF's bundled benefits and the 1% tax exemption make it the most tax-efficient default for salaried employees. CIT is most useful as a top-up for freelancers or business owners who don't have access to an employer scheme, since it lets you claim the retirement deduction independently. EPF remains relevant mainly for people at organisations that haven't migrated to SSF yet.
